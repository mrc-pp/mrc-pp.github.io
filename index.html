Abstract

This letter presents a framework for multi-robot coverage path planning in large, nonconvex Regions of Interest containing obstacles and no-fly zones. The proposed method begins by determining an optimal sweep orientation through the minimum-area enclosing rectangle computed using a rotating-calipers procedure. Using this orientation, a minimum-turn swath generation algorithm produces parallel coverage lines that are clipped against the obstacle-free space and sorted along the field’s minor axis to ensure spatial continuity and reduce turn transitions. Both the outer ROI boundary and all internal exclusion zones are expanded using analytically derived headland buffers, producing a reduced operable region that guarantees sufficient clearance for turning maneuvers and prevents boundary violations. A visibility-roadmap (VRM) structure provides geometric connectivity between disjoint swath segments, enabling feasible transitions that remain entirely inside the buffered region. A modified multiple Traveling Salesman Problem (mTSP) formulation assigns the ordered swaths to robots, balancing workloads and minimizing total mission time. Simulations and real-world experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework significantly reduces the number of turns, energy consumption, and computation time compared to state-of-the-art planners, while maintaining strong scalability across increasing fleet sizes.

Overview & Contributions

MRCPP integrates analytic geometry, graph-based connectivity, and optimization-based allocation to produce efficient coverage paths for non-convex ROIs with obstacles and no-fly zones.

Key Ideas

  • Minimum-area sweep orientation: rotating calipers compute the minimum-area enclosing rectangle to set a globally consistent sweep direction.
  • Minimum-turn swath generation: parallel swaths aligned with the sweep direction, clipped to the ROI, ordered along the minor axis.
  • Headland buffers: buffering the ROI boundary and exclusion zones yields a safe operable region for turns.
  • VRM connectivity: a visibility-roadmap graph connects disjoint swaths with collision-free transitions.
  • Workload-balanced allocation: a modified mTSP assigns swaths to minimize the maximum robot path length.

MRCPP Pipeline

MRCPP follows a geometry-to-optimization workflow: (i) compute a global sweep orientation, (ii) generate parallel swaths in the buffered free space, (iii) connect disjoint swaths using VRM-based transitions, and (iv) allocate swaths to robots via a workload-balanced mTSP formulation.

Benchmark Results

We evaluate MRCPP on multiple benchmark environments including Cape, Complex, Island, Simple, and Rectangle, each containing obstacles and/or no-fly zones. Performance is reported in terms of computation time, path length, and energy consumption using both trajectory-free and trajectory-based estimators. In addition, scalability is assessed in a fixed complex NFZ-constrained environment by increasing the fleet size.

EAMCMP Baseline Comparison

We further compare MRCPP against the EAMCMP baseline across identical benchmark environments. Compared to MRCPP, EAMCMP consistently produces longer execution paths and higher energy consumption. This behavior stems from the absence of an explicit minimum-turn swath orientation, resulting in frequent reorientation maneuvers and increased ferrying distance, particularly in elongated or structured regions. As a result, MRCPP achieves superior energy efficiency and reduced mission completion time.

Real-World Field Experiments

We validate MRCPP with two real-world deployments: (i) aerial coverage using two DJI Mavic Air AAVs (paths uploaded and executed via Litchi), and (ii) aquatic coverage using two ASVs equipped with YSI EXO2 sondes for in situ sensing. The executed coverage paths and the no-fly zone are visualized in the experiment interface and overlaid on satellite imagery.
