Overview

Multi-robot coverage in irregular ROIs becomes inefficient when swaths fragment around obstacles and headings change frequently. MRCPP generates globally aligned swaths and connects disjoint segments with feasible detours, then allocates swaths across robots to reduce mission completion time.

Orientation-optimized swaths

MRCPP computes a minimum-area enclosing rectangle (rotating calipers) to select the sweep direction, then generates parallel swaths aligned with the principal axis.

Headland-safe operable region

The ROI is offset inward and obstacles/no-fly zones are expanded outward using a headland width to guarantee clearance for turning maneuvers and prevent boundary violations.

VRM connectivity + balanced allocation

A visibility roadmap provides collision-free transitions between disjoint swath segments. A modified mTSP balances workload across robots and reduces mission time.

Method

MRCPP integrates geometric reasoning, graph-based connectivity, and optimization-based allocation.

1

Optimal sweep orientation

Compute the minimum-area enclosing rectangle using rotating calipers to determine the sweep axes.

2

Minimum-turn swath generation

Generate parallel swath lines and clip them against the ROI to obtain feasible swath segments.

3

Headland buffers

Offset the ROI inward and expand obstacles/no-fly zones outward to form a reduced operable region for safe turns.

4

Visibility-roadmap (VRM) detours

Use VRM shortest paths whenever straight-line transitions are blocked to maintain collision-free connectivity.

5

Workload-balanced mTSP allocation

Assign ordered swaths to robots by solving a modified mTSP (e.g., using LKH) and generate executable routes.

Results

In simulation and real-world experiments, MRCPP reduces turning effort, energy consumption, and computation time while maintaining scalability across increasing fleet sizes.

Benchmarking

MRCPP is evaluated against state-of-the-art planners in multiple environments with varying geometric complexity and no-fly zone constraints. Metrics include computation time, energy (trajectory-free and trajectory-based), and path length.

  • Fast planning (often sub-second to a few seconds depending on environment)
  • Energy-efficient coverage with balanced distribution across robots
  • Competitive path length with consistent feasibility in constrained ROIs

Scalability

MRCPP demonstrates strong scalability when increasing the number of robots in the same constrained environment by maintaining low computation time and improved average energy per robot through balanced assignment.

Global swath alignment + efficient VRM transitions + mTSP balancing

Real-World Experiments

MRCPP is validated with both aerial (AAV) and aquatic (ASV) deployments.

AAV coverage (DJI Mavic Air)

Two DJI Mavic Air AAVs execute planned coverage paths uploaded to Litchi. Total coverage path length is 615 m (306 m + 309 m), completed in 78 s with average speeds 4.25 m/s and 4.41 m/s.

  • Concurrent execution with globally aligned swaths
  • No-fly zone respected during coverage

ASV coverage (coastal bay)

Two ASVs equipped with YSI EXO2 sondes execute coverage in a coastal bay ROI. Executed path lengths: 138.15 m (ASV1) and 125.71 m (ASV2).

  • Real-time monitoring interface with path overlays
  • Feasible transitions maintained within the operable region
